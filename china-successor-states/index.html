Counterfactual Political Geography

If China Had Split Like the Other Empires

The Qing collapse of 1911 was, like the Ottoman and Austro-Hungarian collapses, a multi-ethnic empire coming apart at its seams. Unlike those two, almost all of the periphery was pulled back together by 1949 — only Outer Mongolia's independence stuck. This maps the near-misses in two layers: six ethnic homelands with real autonomy or independence movements in that 1911–1949 window — and, inside the Han core itself, five Sinitic near-nations, where the languages diverge as far as the Romance family and a 1920s federalist movement briefly made provincial sovereignty look inevitable.

This is a historical thought experiment, not a policy argument — every state below is built from a documented movement (a declared republic, a self-governing warlord regime, a treaty attempt), not from wishful redrawing. Tap a region on the map for its case.

03

The Han fracture lines

dashed on the map · sinitic core

The deeper counterfactual. "Han" bundles together seven-plus Sinitic languages — Wu, Yue, Min, Xiang, Hakka, Gan, Jin — that diverge from Mandarin and from each other roughly as far as the Romance languages do. Europe's logic would have made each a nation. And the 1920s nearly did: the federalist self-government movement (联省自治) had provinces writing their own constitutions until the Northern Expedition buried it in 1926–28 and the party-state won.

The canonical citation is Mao himself — in September 1920 a 26-year-old Mao Zedong argued in Changsha's press for an independent Republic of Hunan, proposing that China dissolve into twenty-seven sovereign states. Hunan then enacted the era's only provincial constitution that actually operated (January 1922). The man who buried Chinese federalism was, six years earlier, its most radical voice.

Hakka: the nation without a map polygon. Any map of this counterfactual hits the same wall — the Hakka ("guest families") are scattered across the highlands of Guangdong, Fujian, Jiangxi, and Taiwan with no contiguous homeland, which is exactly why they appear in the table but not on the map. Their state precedent is the most spectacular on this page: the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom (1851–64) was Hakka-led, held Nanjing for eleven years, and its suppression was the bloodiest civil war in history. What the Hakka built instead of a state is a leadership diaspora — Taiping founder Hong Xiuquan, PLA founding marshal Zhu De, Lee Kuan Yew, and Lee Teng-hui were Hakka, and Deng Xiaoping is reportedly of Hakka descent.

And the Hundred Yue. The Baiyue — the pre-Chinese peoples of the entire southeast coast — were assimilated over two millennia into today's Yue-, Min-, and Wu-speaking populations and the Tai-Kadai peoples like the Zhuang. They aren't a modern group you can map. But one Baiyue polity permanently escaped the Chinese state, and it's on every map already: Việt Nam is the same word as Yue (越).

04

Method & exclusions

The rule for the solid regions: a documented, named political entity — a declared republic, a self-governing warlord regime, a treaty negotiation — in the 1911–1949 window. The dashed regions use a looser but real bar: a distinct Sinitic language plus a period political anchor (a constitution, a breakaway government, a de facto separate administration). Everything below failed one of those tests or was folded into a listed state.

Miao / Hmong (11.1M today)
No unified territory — scattered across mountain ranges in five provinces and into Vietnam, Laos, Thailand as the Hmong diaspora. No 1920s political entity to anchor a state on.
Yi (9.8M today)
Same problem: highland clan federations across Yunnan, Sichuan, Guizhou, never unified into one polity, no warlord-era claim.
Koreans (Yanbian)
Real irredentist pull toward Korea existed, but the territory and population are small enough that they're folded into the Manchuria case above rather than treated as a seventh state.
Tujia (9.9M today)
Long assimilated and dispersed through Hunan/Hubei/Chongqing with no distinct territorial claim in this period.
Sichuan
Fragmented among its own warlord cliques for the whole era — but it speaks Southwestern Mandarin. Regionalism without a linguistic nation underneath it, so it fails the dashed-region bar.
Yunnan under Long Yun
Genuinely near-independent 1927–45 (own provincial currency, French-Indochina rail link) until Chiang's 1945 coup removed Long Yun — but it was a Han-run multi-ethnic province, not a national movement; its internal claims (Yi, Dai, Bai) never unified.
Gan (Jiangxi, ~22M speakers)
A distinct Sinitic language that never produced a period political anchor — no constitution, no breakaway government. Language alone doesn't clear the bar.

Why the population numbers in §02 aren't the answer to "what would it be composed of." The 2020 census counts each ethnicity across the whole PRC today — after decades of state-directed Han settlement reshaped exactly the regions in question. Xinjiang is roughly 42% Han today; Inner Mongolia is roughly 79% Han. Both shares are a post-1949 policy outcome, not an early-20th-century fact. A state actually founded in the 1911–1949 window would have looked far more Uyghur/Kazakh in the Tarim and Junggar basins, and far more Mongol across the steppe, than either region does now — closer to the composition described for each state above.

Guangxi is the outlier on the other end: its independence precedent (Nong Zhigao, 1042–53) predates the 1911 window by nine centuries. It's included because the Zhuang are linguistically Tai-Kadai — kin to Thai and Lao, not to Chinese — which is the deepest-rooted distinctiveness on this list, even without a 20th-century movement to point to.
